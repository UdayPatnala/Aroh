Every architectural decision within AROH must contribute to a stable, scalable, maintainable, secure, and future-ready ecosystem. Architecture exists to maximize long-term sustainability while minimizing unnecessary complexity and operational cost.

The ecosystem follows a modular monorepo architecture where multiple products share a common platform foundation while remaining independently deployable and maintainable. Shared capabilities are centralized to eliminate duplication and ensure consistency across all products.

The architecture is organized into distinct layers with clearly defined responsibilities. Presentation, application logic, domain logic, shared services, infrastructure, and data storage remain separated to improve maintainability, testing, and scalability.

The backend is the authoritative source of truth. Authentication, authorization, business rules, permissions, transactions, validation, content management, and persistent state are enforced exclusively by backend services. Frontend applications consume and present validated data without assuming authority over business logic.

Every shared capability is implemented as a platform service rather than being embedded within individual products. Authentication, user management, notifications, CMS, search, scheduling, asset management, analytics, membership, and the Aros economy are ecosystem services accessible through standardized interfaces.

The ecosystem adopts an event-driven communication model. Significant system actions publish standardized events that may be consumed by other services without creating direct dependencies. This approach reduces coupling, improves extensibility, and enables future integrations.

Every service exposes clearly documented contracts through standardized APIs. API contracts remain stable, versioned, predictable, and backward compatible whenever practical to prevent breaking dependent systems.

The architecture favors configuration-driven behavior over hardcoded implementation. Platform behavior, permissions, feature availability, navigation, scheduling, branding, content, integrations, and environment-specific settings should be managed through configuration whenever practical.

Reusable packages form the foundation of the ecosystem. Shared UI components, design tokens, utilities, validation schemas, API clients, hooks, business models, SDK functionality, and common services are maintained as centralized packages consumed by every product.

Dependency direction always flows inward. Higher-level modules may depend on lower-level abstractions, but core domain logic must remain independent of presentation frameworks, deployment platforms, and infrastructure implementations.

Security is integrated into every architectural layer. Every service validates authentication, authorization, data integrity, request authenticity, input sanitization, and permission boundaries before executing protected operations.

Scalability is achieved through loose coupling, modular services, reusable infrastructure, standardized interfaces, and clearly defined ownership of responsibilities. New products should integrate through extension rather than modification of existing systems.

The architecture prioritizes horizontal expansion of capabilities without requiring significant restructuring. Future products, services, plugins, APIs, and platform features should register themselves using standardized extension mechanisms while preserving compatibility with existing modules.

Administrative functionality is role-driven rather than route-driven. Administrative capabilities become available only after backend authorization confirms sufficient privileges. Separate administrative applications or authentication mechanisms are intentionally avoided unless future requirements objectively justify them.

The Aros economy follows immutable transaction principles. Every balance modification is represented by an auditable transaction. Direct manipulation of stored balances is prohibited to preserve financial integrity, traceability, and recoverability.

Content management follows a centralized architecture. Dynamic content including announcements, events, promotional material, homepage sections, product highlights, and scheduled publications are managed through a unified CMS instead of hardcoded application content.

Observability is considered an architectural responsibility. Logging, monitoring, analytics, metrics, auditing, and health reporting are integrated throughout the ecosystem to simplify maintenance, debugging, performance optimization, and operational visibility.

Every architectural component must satisfy the requirements of simplicity, consistency, modularity, security, accessibility, maintainability, scalability, and performance before being accepted into the ecosystem.

Architectural changes are governed by the Architecture Freeze Rule. Approved architectural decisions remain stable throughout implementation unless an objectively superior alternative demonstrates measurable improvements in maintainability, scalability, security, performance, developer experience, operational cost, or long-term sustainability. Any approved change must be propagated throughout all dependent documentation before implementation continues.

The architecture is designed to support the complete Phase 0, Phase 1, and Phase 2 roadmap while exposing clearly defined extension points for documented Phase 3 capabilities without requiring disruptive redesigns or introducing technical debt.
